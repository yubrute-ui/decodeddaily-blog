## Why Your Brain Is Obsessed With "What Just Happened"

You check your investment portfolio after a brutal week in the market. The numbers are red. Suddenly, without consciously deciding to, you start picturing more red — next month, next year, maybe forever. You weren't thinking that way on Monday. But now? It feels obvious that things are only going to get worse.

Welcome to recency bias — one of the most quietly powerful forces shaping your financial decisions, career choices, and everyday judgments.

---

## What Recency Bias Actually Is

Recency bias is the cognitive tendency to overweight recent events when predicting or evaluating the future. Instead of pulling from the full range of available information, your brain leans heavily on what happened *last* — last week, last quarter, last conversation.

It's not a character flaw. It's a cognitive shortcut, and for most of human history, it was genuinely useful. If you ate a berry that made you sick yesterday, treating that as highly relevant information today made perfect sense. Fresh data was often the most reliable data.

The problem is that modern life — especially investing, career planning, and economic forecasting — demands a much longer frame of reference than your brain naturally offers.

---

## The Availability Heuristic: Recency's Close Cousin

Recency bias rarely travels alone. It works hand in hand with what psychologists call the **availability heuristic** — the mental shortcut where you judge the probability of something based on how easily an example comes to mind.

Recent events are, by definition, easy to recall. They're vivid. They're emotionally charged. They feel *present*. So your brain quietly elevates them in importance, treating them less like one data point and more like the whole story.

When grocery prices spike for several months in a row, the emotional weight of that experience makes it feel like prices will rise indefinitely — even when historical patterns suggest otherwise. The recent spike is mentally available. The decades of fluctuation that preceded it are not.

---

## Real-Life Examples You Probably Recognize

**In investing:** After a market downturn, many people shift toward overly conservative portfolios — or sell altogether — right before a recovery. After a bull run, many people pour money into high-risk assets at the worst possible moment. Both moves are driven by projecting the recent past onto the future.

**At work:** Have a string of difficult Mondays? By Wednesday you're already dreading next week. A few successful presentations and you start overestimating how prepared you are for the next big one. Your most recent performance — good or bad — hijacks your self-assessment.

**In relationships:** One great week with a partner can make you forget months of unresolved tension. One bad argument can color your perception of an otherwise healthy relationship. Recency shapes the emotional filter through which you interpret everything.

---

## Why This Is So Hard to Catch in the Moment

The tricky part is that recency bias doesn't feel like bias. It feels like *clarity*. When a trend is fresh and emotionally loaded, the conclusion it points to feels obvious and well-reasoned. You're not being impulsive — you're "reading the room."

That's exactly what makes it dangerous. Decisions that feel grounded in reality may actually be grounded in a very narrow window of reality.

---

## How to Push Back Against It

The antidote isn't complex, but it does require deliberate effort. Here are a few practical ways to counter recency bias in your own thinking:

- **Zoom out on your timeline.** Before making a major decision based on recent events, ask yourself: what does the last five years look like? The last decade? A single chapter rarely tells the whole story.

- **Ask the "trend or moment" question.** When you catch yourself making a prediction, pause and ask: *Am I responding to a sustained pattern, or just a recent spike?* One bad month of sales data is not a collapse. One great quarter is not a guaranteed trajectory.

- **Seek disconfirming data deliberately.** Your brain will surface examples that match your recent experience. Force yourself to look for counterexamples — times when the pattern broke, when things recovered, when the obvious outcome didn't materialize.

- **Write it down before you decide.** Journaling your reasoning before a big decision creates a record you can actually examine. It's much harder to spot the influence of recency in your head than on paper.

- **Talk to someone with a longer view.** A financial advisor, mentor, or even a trusted friend who hasn't been inside your recent emotional experience can offer the perspective your brain isn't naturally generating.

---

Your brain is not broken. It's doing exactly what it evolved to do — prioritizing the fresh, the vivid, and the emotionally relevant. But the future rarely looks exactly like the last few weeks. Recognizing that gap, and choosing to look at the longer arc, is one of the most rational things you can do.
