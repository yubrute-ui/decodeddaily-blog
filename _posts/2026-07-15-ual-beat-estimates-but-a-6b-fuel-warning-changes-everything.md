## What Is Read-Through Analysis — and Why Every Investor Should Know It

When United Airlines reports earnings, most headlines focus on the beat or miss. But savvy investors ask a different question: *what does this tell me about everyone else?* That's the core of **read-through analysis** — using one company's results to infer what might be happening across an entire industry.

Let's start with today's case, then zoom out to the technique itself.

---

## The United Airlines Setup (Today's Case)

United Airlines (UAL) posted a strong second quarter, beating Wall Street estimates across nearly every segment — premium cabins, corporate travel, basic economy, domestic routes, and international (as of the Q2 2026 earnings release, July 15, 2026). The stock was trading around $120.97, with a market cap of approximately $39.26 billion, up roughly 36.7% over the prior 52 weeks (as of mid-July 2026).

Impressive. But buried in the same report was a flag that deserves equal attention: United projected roughly **$6 billion in added fuel costs** ahead — their own forward guidance, not an industry-wide statistic.

Here's the tension: demand is clearly strong. But fuel is airlines' largest variable cost. Even with revenue firing on all cylinders, a $6 billion cost headwind can compress margins quickly. The real question isn't whether UAL beat last quarter — it's whether premium and corporate revenue growth is durable enough to absorb that kind of pressure going forward.

That tension is *exactly* what read-through analysis is designed to surface.

---

## So What Is Read-Through Analysis, Exactly?

Read-through analysis is the practice of taking disclosed information from one company — earnings, guidance, cost warnings, demand commentary — and reasoning about what it *implies* for peer companies, suppliers, or customers that haven't yet reported.

It works because most businesses don't operate in isolation. Airlines share the same jet fuel markets, the same labor pools, and the same macro demand environment. When one major carrier flags $6 billion in added fuel costs, that's not just a UAL story. It's potentially a Delta (DAL) story. An American Airlines (AAL) story. A story about airplane manufacturers and fuel hedging desks across the sector.

---

## How to Apply Read-Through Analysis Step by Step

**1. Identify the signal — and classify it.**
Is the disclosed item demand-driven (e.g., "corporate travel is surging") or cost-driven (e.g., "fuel costs are spiking")? Demand signals often read through *positively* to peers. Cost signals usually read through *negatively* — but the direction can vary depending on who's hedged and who isn't.

**2. Ask: is this company-specific or industry-wide?**
UAL's $6 billion fuel projection is forward guidance from their own operations. But if jet fuel prices are rising in the open market, DAL and AAL face the same input costs. That's a sector-wide read-through. By contrast, if UAL's fuel bill is higher because they expanded routes aggressively, that's more company-specific — and the read-through to peers is weaker.

**3. Map the peer landscape.**
As of mid-July 2026, DAL was trading near $85.96 (up ~57% over 52 weeks, market cap ~$56.53 billion), and AAL was near $15.63 (up ~27.4%, market cap ~$10.34 billion). Three airlines, three very different scales and balance sheets. When applying a read-through, scale matters — a $6 billion fuel cost is proportionally more painful for a smaller-cap carrier.

**4. Weight the offsetting factors.**
Read-through analysis isn't about assuming peers will mirror the reporting company exactly. It's about asking: *who has the revenue mix or cost structure to absorb this, and who doesn't?* A carrier with a higher share of premium and corporate revenue may be better insulated from fuel pressure than one dependent on discount leisure travelers.

**5. Hold your conclusion loosely.**
A read-through is a hypothesis, not a forecast. It narrows your research focus. It does not replace reading each company's own filings.

## Where Else Does This Technique Apply?

Read-through analysis extends well beyond airlines:

- **Semiconductors:** When TSMC comments on AI chip demand, that reads through to Nvidia, AMD, and their customers.
- **Retail:** A Target margin warning on inventory can signal pressure for Walmart and Costco before they report.
- **Banks:** When JPMorgan flags rising credit card delinquencies, community banks and fintech lenders are worth watching.
- **Energy:** An Exxon refining margin update reads through to Chevron and independent refiners.

The common thread: look for *shared inputs, shared customers, or shared macro exposure*.

---

## Practical Tips for Using Read-Through Analysis

- **Calendar your peers.** When a major company in any sector reports, immediately note which peers report next — those gaps are your read-through window.
- **Separate guidance from results.** Backward-looking beats are interesting. Forward-looking cost or demand commentary is where read-throughs live.
- **Check hedge positions.** In commodities-heavy industries (airlines, shipping, utilities), hedging differences can make the same macro signal hit companies very differently.
- **Don't over-extrapolate.** "UAL flagged fuel costs, therefore short AAL" is not an investment thesis. It's a starting point for deeper diligence.
