## Why the Smartest People Are Often the Worst Explainers

You've probably sat through a presentation where the speaker clearly knew their subject inside and out — and yet you left more confused than when you walked in. Or maybe you've been on the other side: patiently explaining something you know deeply, only to watch the other person's eyes glaze over. Neither person is being careless. Both are likely caught in the same psychological trap: the Curse of Knowledge.

## What the Curse of Knowledge Actually Is

The Curse of Knowledge is a cognitive bias that describes a simple but frustrating phenomenon — once you know something, your brain struggles to remember what it was like *not* to know it. Your knowledge becomes invisible to you. The confusion you once felt fades from memory, and the gaps you had to bridge disappear from your awareness entirely.

This means that when you try to explain something, you unconsciously skip over the very steps that made it click for you in the first place. You assume shared context that simply isn't there. And the more deeply you know something, the worse this gets.

## The Assumptions You Forgot You Were Making

Think about a software developer explaining their code to a non-technical manager. They might say, "It's just a REST API call" — a sentence that feels self-explanatory to them. But "REST API" carries an entire mental framework that the listener may not have. The developer has forgotten that they once had to learn what that meant, because learning it is now so far in the rearview mirror it's effectively invisible.

This happens in everyday conversations too. A parent trying to help a child with math might struggle to explain why carrying a digit works the way it does — not because the math is actually hard, but because the parent no longer has access to the mental state of not understanding it. The confusion has been overwritten.

The bias was named and studied in the context of economics and behavioral science, and the concept has since been widely applied in fields ranging from education and UX design to management and marketing. The central challenge it identifies is consistent: **knowledge creates a one-way door**. You can walk through it, but you can't fully walk back.

## Why Repeating Yourself Louder Never Works

When someone looks confused, most people's instinct is to repeat what they just said — sometimes slower, sometimes louder — as if the problem were a volume issue. But the real gap is almost never in delivery. It's in the unspoken assumptions the speaker forgot they were making.

When you repeat yourself without adjusting the content, you're essentially re-serving the same meal to someone who couldn't taste it the first time. The issue isn't the presentation. It's the missing ingredients — the foundational context that felt so obvious you didn't think to include it.

The better question to ask yourself isn't *"how do I say this more clearly?"* but rather *"what am I assuming they already know?"* That shift in framing is where real communication breakthroughs happen.

## Where This Bias Shows Up Most

The Curse of Knowledge surfaces in more places than most people realize:

- **The workplace**: Experienced employees who onboard new hires and skip over "basics" that aren't actually basic
- **Education**: Teachers who have taught a concept so many times that the hard parts feel obvious
- **Product design**: Designers who build interfaces that make sense to them but confuse first-time users
- **Relationships**: Partners who assume emotional context that was never explicitly communicated

In each case, the person with more knowledge isn't being condescending or lazy. They've simply lost access to their earlier mental state.

## Practical Tips to Break the Curse

You can't unlearn what you know — but you can build habits that work around the bias:

- **Ask "what would a newcomer need to know first?"** before you explain anything. Work backward from your knowledge to the baseline.
- **Test your explanation on someone outside the field.** If they can follow it, you've actually communicated.
- **Welcome confusion as information.** When someone looks lost, treat it as a signal to find the hidden assumption, not to repeat yourself.
- **Use analogies that live in their world**, not yours. A good analogy borrows from the listener's existing knowledge, not the speaker's.
- **Remember your own beginner moments.** Actively journaling about when *you* didn't understand something can help keep that empathy accessible.

The Curse of Knowledge isn't a flaw in intelligence — it's actually a byproduct of learning well. The goal isn't to know less. It's to stay genuinely curious about what the person across from you doesn't yet know, and build the bridge from there.
