A doctor says it. A CEO says it. And suddenly — you stop thinking for yourself.

That's **authority bias**: the tendency to over-trust the opinion of someone we perceive as an authority, and to weight their judgment far more heavily than the actual evidence behind it.

## The mental shortcut

Your brain is an efficiency machine. Evaluating every claim from scratch is expensive, so it takes shortcuts. One of the most powerful is: *"They have the title, so they must be right."* No verification. No pushback. Just automatic trust.

That shortcut is often useful — we can't personally audit every pilot, surgeon, or engineer we rely on. But the same reflex misfires constantly:

- A **financial analyst** calls a market move, and millions reposition without reading a single chart.
- A **doctor** recommends something off-script, and patients comply without a single question.
- A **manager** states an opinion in a meeting, and the room stops generating alternatives.

In each case, the *title* did the convincing — not the argument.

## Why it's dangerous

Authority is a **signal**, not **proof**. A credential raises the prior probability that someone is right; it doesn't guarantee it. The failure mode is when the signal *replaces* your own evaluation entirely — you outsource the thinking and stop checking whether the reasoning actually holds.

The most dangerous version is the quiet one: *"They know, so I don't need to."* That's the moment you go from using an expert as an input to using them as a substitute for your own judgment.

## How to push back

You don't need to distrust experts — you need to keep your own reasoning switched on:

1. **Separate the claim from the credential.** Ask: *what's the actual evidence here?* If the answer is only "because they said so," that's a flag.
2. **Notice when you go quiet.** If a title makes you stop asking questions, that's authority bias doing its work.
3. **Let authority raise your attention, not lower your scrutiny.** An expert's take is a great reason to look closer — not a reason to look away.

Next time credentials impress you, run the test: *strip the title — does the argument still stand on its own?*
