## The 1920 Army Study That Exposed How Your Brain Gets Fooled Every Day

There is a quiet glitch running in the background of nearly every judgment you make about other people. It does not announce itself. It does not ask for permission. And for a long time, nobody had a name for it — until an American psychologist ran a deceptively simple study on U.S. Army soldiers and uncovered something that would change how we understand human perception forever.

## What Thorndike Actually Found

In 1920, psychologist Edward Thorndike published a paper titled *A Constant Error in Psychological Ratings*. The setup was straightforward: he asked U.S. Army commanding officers to evaluate soldiers across several traits that were designed to be logically independent of one another — physical bearing, intelligence, leadership ability, and character.

The results should have shown a scattered, varied picture. A soldier might rank high in physical bearing but average in leadership. Another might demonstrate strong character but middling intelligence. That is what you would expect if officers were genuinely evaluating each quality on its own merits.

That is not what happened.

Thorndike found the ratings were implausibly, suspiciously correlated. Officers who saw a soldier as physically impressive also rated him as more intelligent, more ethical, and a stronger leader — even though physical appearance carries no logical information about a person's moral character or cognitive ability. The opposite pattern held too: soldiers who made a poor first physical impression were dragged down across the board.

Thorndike concluded that officers were not actually making four separate evaluations. They were forming one overarching global impression of a person and then letting that single impression quietly color every specific judgment that followed. He called it a *constant error* — a systematic, predictable distortion baked into the rating process itself. This is the founding moment of what we now call the **Halo Effect**.

## Why Your Brain Does This

The Halo Effect is not a character flaw or a sign of low intelligence. It is, in many ways, a feature of how the human brain manages the overwhelming complexity of social judgment. Sizing up another person fully and accurately takes enormous cognitive effort. Forming one strong impression and extending it outward is faster and cheaper.

The problem is that the brain does not always flag when it is taking this shortcut. The extended judgment feels just as deliberate and evidence-based as a careful, trait-by-trait evaluation would. You genuinely believe you are assessing someone's intelligence independently — and you are not.

## The Halo Travels Beyond Its Source

Here is where the Halo Effect moves from an interesting historical footnote into something genuinely worth paying attention to in your daily life.

Consider a celebrated scientist who has spent decades earning a reputation for rigorous, groundbreaking work in their specific field. That reputation is a halo — and it is a deserved one, within its domain. Now imagine that same scientist steps outside their field and makes a confident prediction about financial markets, geopolitical strategy, or nutrition. Many people will receive that prediction with the same deference they would give to the scientist's peer-reviewed research.

Not because there is financial or strategic evidence supporting the claim. Because the halo travels with the person.

The same dynamic plays out with tech founders, celebrity athletes, and influential public figures of all kinds. Impressive achievement in one area casts a glow over every other area, and that glow can be nearly invisible to the people standing in it.

## A More Grounded Way to Evaluate Claims

Awareness alone will not eliminate the Halo Effect — Thorndike's work made clear it is a deeply embedded cognitive pattern — but it can meaningfully slow it down. A few practical habits help:

- **Separate the domain from the claim.** Before accepting someone's opinion, ask explicitly: is this their area of demonstrated expertise, or are they operating on borrowed credibility?
- **Look for the actual evidence.** Ask what specific evidence supports this particular claim, independent of who is making it.
- **Notice the impression before it spreads.** When you feel an immediate, strong positive reaction to someone, treat that as a signal to slow down your subsequent judgments rather than speed them up.
- **Apply the same scrutiny to people you admire.** The Halo Effect is strongest where you least expect to find a bias — in your assessments of people you already respect.

Thorndike called it a constant error for a reason. It is not occasional or situational. It is woven into the default setting. The first step toward correcting it is knowing, clearly, where it came from — and recognizing it the next time an impressive person asks you to trust them somewhere they have never actually earned that trust.
