## You're Not Looking for the Truth — You're Looking to Be Right

There's a quiet habit most of us carry without realizing it. When we form a belief — about a person, a decision, or an idea — our minds don't stay neutral. Instead, they start working like a filter, letting in the information that fits and quietly pushing aside the information that doesn't. Psychologists call this **confirmation bias**, and it's one of the most pervasive patterns in human thinking.

Understanding confirmation bias isn't just an academic exercise. It shapes how you vote, how you invest, how you judge people, and how you make decisions every single day.

---

## What Confirmation Bias Actually Looks Like

At its core, confirmation bias is the tendency to seek out, favor, and remember information that confirms what we already believe — while discounting or ignoring evidence that contradicts it.

Here's where it gets interesting: it doesn't require dishonesty or stubbornness. It can happen consciously, but it very often works below the level of awareness. You might genuinely believe you're being open-minded while your attention quietly gravitates toward only the sources that agree with you.

Some everyday examples:

- **Investing:** You become convinced a particular stock is going to rise. Suddenly, every bullish headline feels like validation, while bearish analysis gets skimmed or dismissed as pessimism.
- **Relationships:** You decide someone at work doesn't like you. From that point on, their neutral expressions feel cold, their brief replies feel rude, and any friendliness gets explained away as performance.
- **Health decisions:** You've read that a certain diet is the healthiest. You unconsciously remember every article that supports it and forget the ones that raise concerns.

None of these people are trying to deceive themselves. That's precisely what makes confirmation bias so difficult to catch.

---

## Why Your Brain Does This

The human brain processes an enormous amount of information every day. To manage that load, it relies on shortcuts — and one of those shortcuts is leaning on existing beliefs as a framework for interpreting new information.

When something confirms what you already think, it feels smooth and easy to process. When something challenges your belief, it creates a kind of mental friction. Over time, the brain tends to prefer the path of least resistance.

There's also an identity dimension. Many of our beliefs aren't just opinions — they're tied to who we think we are, the groups we belong to, and the decisions we've already made. Questioning those beliefs can feel like a threat, not just an intellectual exercise.

---

## The Slow Distortion of Certainty

One of the more subtle consequences of confirmation bias is that it makes your beliefs feel *more certain over time* — not necessarily because the evidence has grown stronger, but because you've been selectively exposed to supporting evidence.

You don't just ignore contradictory information once. You do it repeatedly, across months or years. Meanwhile, the confirming information accumulates in your memory. Eventually, the belief feels rock-solid — fortified not by rigorous evaluation, but by a long pattern of selective attention.

This is part of why it's genuinely hard to change someone's mind with facts alone. By the time you introduce conflicting evidence, their belief may already feel like established certainty.

---

## How to Push Back Against It

Confirmation bias can't be eliminated entirely — it's deeply woven into how cognition works. But it can be reduced with deliberate practice.

**Practical tips to try:**

1. **Notice the friction.** When a piece of information makes you uncomfortable or defensive, pause instead of dismissing it. That discomfort is worth examining — it often signals a real challenge to your thinking.
2. **Actively seek disconfirmation.** Before finalizing an important decision, ask yourself: *What would prove me wrong here?* Then genuinely look for it.
3. **Slow down with opposing views.** You likely skim articles you disagree with. Try reading one fully before forming a response.
4. **Separate your belief from your identity.** Remind yourself that updating a view isn't a personal failure — it's what good thinking actually looks like.
5. **Talk to people who genuinely disagree with you.** Not to argue, but to understand the strongest version of the opposing position.

The goal isn't to doubt everything. It's to make sure the confidence you feel in your beliefs has been *earned* — not just accumulated through selective attention. The next time a new fact creates friction in your mind, that might be exactly the moment worth sitting with.
