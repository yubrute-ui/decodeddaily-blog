## The 1920 Study That Proved Your Brain Has Been Judging People Wrong All Along

You think you evaluate people objectively. You weigh their qualities, consider the evidence, and form a balanced opinion. That's what most of us believe about ourselves. But nearly a century ago, a psychologist ran a simple experiment on U.S. Army officers and exposed a deeply inconvenient truth: our brains don't actually work that way.

## What Thorndike Actually Found

In 1920, American psychologist Edward Thorndike published a paper titled *A Constant Error in Psychological Ratings*. The study itself was elegantly straightforward. Thorndike asked commanding officers in the U.S. Army to rate their soldiers across several qualities that were deliberately chosen to be logically independent of one another: physical bearing, intelligence, leadership ability, and character.

The assumption was simple. A soldier's posture and physique shouldn't tell you much about his moral character. His leadership skills shouldn't logically predict his raw intelligence. These are separate dimensions of a person.

But the ratings didn't look separate at all.

Officers who rated a soldier highly on physical bearing also gave him strong marks on intelligence, character, and leadership — and vice versa. The correlations were implausibly high for traits that have no inherent logical connection. Thorndike concluded that the officers weren't actually evaluating each quality on its own merits. Instead, they were forming one broad, global impression of a person first, and then letting that single impression color every specific judgment they made afterward.

He called it a *constant error* in psychological ratings. Today, we know it as the **Halo Effect**.

## Why the Halo Effect Is So Hard to Catch

The insidious thing about this cognitive bias is that it doesn't feel like a bias. It feels like observation.

When a well-dressed, confident speaker takes the stage, the thought isn't *I am now assuming this person is intelligent because they look polished.* The thought is simply *This person seems really sharp.* The halo is invisible precisely because it works before conscious reasoning kicks in.

This is why the effect shows up consistently across so many real-world contexts:

- **Job interviews:** A candidate with a firm handshake and strong eye contact may be rated as more competent, even before answering a single question.
- **Classrooms:** Teachers who perceive a student as physically attractive or well-behaved may unconsciously rate their academic work more favorably.
- **Everyday social life:** Someone who is charming and funny at a first meeting often gets the benefit of the doubt on unrelated qualities — reliability, honesty, even competence.

In each case, one salient positive trait creates a mental glow that radiates outward, illuminating traits the brain hasn't actually evaluated yet.

## The Evolutionary Logic Behind an Irrational Shortcut

To be fair to your brain, there's a reason this shortcut exists. Humans are social animals who historically needed to make rapid judgments about other people — are they a threat? An ally? A potential mate? Processing every trait of every person you encounter from scratch would be cognitively exhausting.

So the brain learned to use a proxy: if someone scores high on one salient, easily observable quality, assume the rest of the package is similarly good. It's efficient. In many ancestral contexts, it probably worked well enough.

The problem is that "well enough for a quick social read" is very different from "reliable enough to evaluate a job candidate, a student, or a political leader." The modern world asks our brains to make high-stakes judgments in situations they weren't originally designed to handle — and the halo effect quietly follows us into those situations uninvited.

## Practical Tips to Counter the Halo Effect

You can't eliminate this bias entirely, but you can build habits that slow it down:

1. **Name the first impression.** When someone impresses you immediately, pause and ask yourself: *Which specific trait actually earned this reaction?* Naming it makes the halo visible.
2. **Evaluate traits separately and sequentially.** In formal settings like interviews or performance reviews, assess one quality at a time before moving to the next. Mixing them together invites the halo to do the averaging for you.
3. **Seek disconfirming evidence.** Actively look for information that contradicts your initial impression. This isn't cynicism — it's accuracy.
4. **Use structured criteria.** Whether you're hiring, grading, or giving feedback, predefined rubrics reduce the room your overall impression has to bleed into specific ratings.

Thorndike's officers weren't bad people or unusually shallow thinkers. They were normal human beings using a normal cognitive shortcut. The difference now is that you know the shortcut exists — which means you finally have a chance to catch it.
